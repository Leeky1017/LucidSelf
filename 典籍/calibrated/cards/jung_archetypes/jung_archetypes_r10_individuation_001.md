### Core Thesis in One Sentence
> Individuation is the process by which an individual differentiates from collective norms and integrates unconscious contents to become a unique, whole personality—not through ego-inflation but through realization of the self as totality of conscious and unconscious psyche.

## L1 - Deep Comprehension Layer

### L1.1 Source Text and Context

**Source Text** (Definition, [494]-[498]):
> Individuation means becoming an "in-dividual," and, in so far as "individuality" embraces our innermost, last, and incomparable uniqueness, it also implies becoming one's own self. We could therefore translate individuation as "coming to selfhood" or "self-realization."

**Source Text** (Self as Totality, [503]-[507]):
> The self is not only the centre but also the whole circumference which embraces both conscious and unconscious; it is the centre of this totality, just as the ego is the centre of consciousness.

**Source Text** (Not Ego-Inflation, [512]-[515]):
> Individuation does not shut one out from the world, but gathers the world to oneself. [...] A conscious distinction must therefore be made between the ego and the self, the ego being only the subject of my consciousness, while the self is the subject of my total psyche, which also includes the unconscious.

### L1.2 Historical Background and Academic Lineage

| Dimension | Content |

### L1.3 Author's Intent and Rhetorical Analysis

| Dimension | Analysis |
|:---|:---|
| Author's intent | To define individuation precisely; to distinguish it from ego-inflation or social isolation; to establish the self as totality (not just ego); to show individuation as life's central psychological task. |
| Rhetorical strategy | (1) Etymological analysis (in-dividual = undivided); (2) Contrast with common misunderstandings (not ego-centered, not antisocial); (3) Geometric metaphor (self as center AND circumference); (4) Ego-self distinction. |
| Implicit assumptions | Self exists as psychic reality beyond ego; development toward wholeness is possible and desirable; ego-self distinction is meaningful and operationalizable; individuation is a natural process that can be supported. |
